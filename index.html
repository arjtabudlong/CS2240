Biography

Jose Rizal, born on June 19, 1861, in Calamba, Laguna, Philippines, was a polymath, nationalist, and one of the most prominent figures in the Philippine Revolution against Spanish colonial rule.

He received his early education in Calamba and later attended the Ateneo Municipal de Manila for his secondary education before pursuing medicine at the University of Santo Tomas and Philosophy and Letters at the Universidad Central de Madrid. Rizal's exposure to European liberalism and nationalism, as well as his experiences of discrimination in Spanish colonial society, influenced his nationalist sentiments, leading him to write his first novel, "Noli Me Tangere" (Touch Me Not), which criticized the Spanish friars and colonial administration in the Philippines.

Upon returning to the Philippines in 1887, he founded the La Liga Filipina, advocating for political reforms and equality, but was subsequently arrested and exiled to Dapitan in Mindanao in 1892. During his exile, he continued his scientific and literary pursuits, establishing a school, practicing medicine, and contributing to various fields of knowledge. He also wrote his second novel, "El Filibusterismo" (The Reign of Greed), which further criticized Spanish abuses and corruption. Though initially hesitant about armed rebellion, Rizal supported the Philippine Revolution when the Katipunan began an uprising against Spanish rule in 1896. However, his efforts to seek reforms through peaceful means were unsuccessful, and he was arrested and tried by a Spanish military court, ultimately being found guilty of rebellion, sedition, and conspiracy.

On December 30, 1896, he was executed by firing squad in Manila, sparking outrage among Filipinos and further fueling the revolution. Jose Rizal's martyrdom galvanized the Philippine Revolution, leading to the eventual independence of the Philippines from Spanish colonial rule. He is celebrated as a national hero, and his writings, particularly his novels, continue to be studied as foundational texts in Philippine literature. Rizal's ideas of nationalism, reform, and education remain influential in Philippine society, inspiring generations of Filipinos.

Most Notable Works

Noli Me Tangere

"Noli Me Tangere" is a novel written by Jose Rizal, published in 1887, during the Spanish colonial period in the Philippines. The title, Latin for "Touch Me Not," is derived from the Gospel of John in the Bible. The novel is a social commentary that exposes the abuses of the Spanish friars and the injustices under colonial rule. It follows the story of Crisostomo Ibarra, a young Filipino returning from Europe, as he navigates the corrupt and oppressive society of his homeland. Through its characters and plot, "Noli Me Tangere" sheds light on various issues such as colonial exploitation, social inequality, religious hypocrisy, and the need for reform. The novel sparked controversy and was met with resistance from Spanish authorities, but it also inspired nationalist sentiments among Filipinos and contributed to the awakening of Filipino consciousness. Today, "Noli Me Tangere" is considered a seminal work in Philippine literature and is often studied in schools for its historical significance and its enduring relevance to Philippine society.

El Filibusterismo

"El Filibusterismo" is the second novel written by Jose Rizal, published in 1891 as a sequel to his earlier work, "Noli Me Tangere." The title, Spanish for "The Reign of Greed" or "The Subversive," reflects the darker and more revolutionary tone of the novel compared to its predecessor. Set in the Philippines under Spanish colonial rule, "El Filibusterismo" continues the narrative of the characters introduced in "Noli Me Tangere" and delves deeper into the themes of social injustice, oppression, and the struggle for reform. The protagonist, Crisostomo Ibarra, returns under the guise of Simoun, a wealthy jeweler, seeking revenge against the corrupt ruling class and plotting a revolution to overthrow the colonial regime. Through its vivid portrayal of Filipino society and its scathing critique of Spanish abuses, "El Filibusterismo" serves as a powerful indictment of colonialism and exploitation. Today, "El Filibusterismo" is studied alongside its predecessor as a cornerstone of Philippine literature and as a testament to Rizal's enduring influence on Filipino nationalism and identity.
